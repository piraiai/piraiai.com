What is Social Desirability Bias in Surveys?

Arun Raj•July 18, 2026•3 min read•Glossary
Definition

Social desirability bias is a form of response bias in which survey respondents answer questions in ways they believe are socially acceptable, morally correct, or positively viewed by others, rather than providing answers that accurately reflect their true opinions or behaviours.

When Social Desirability Bias Occurs

Social desirability bias is most prevalent in surveys covering sensitive topics where respondents may feel judged for their honest answers. High-risk areas include health behaviours, financial behaviour, political and social attitudes, workplace conduct, and treatment of others. The more a topic touches on moral judgment or social norms, the higher the risk of social desirability distortion.

The mode of survey administration also affects social desirability levels. Interviewer-administered surveys (phone, in-person) show higher social desirability bias than self-administered surveys (online, paper) because the physical or verbal presence of another person amplifies social pressure. This is one reason online surveys produce more honest responses to sensitive questions than phone surveys on the same topics.

Effects on Survey Measurement

Social desirability bias produces systematic distortion in a predictable direction: measures of positive behaviours and attitudes are overreported, while measures of negative behaviours and stigmatised attitudes are underreported. For CX and employee surveys, social desirability bias often inflates satisfaction scores when surveys are not fully anonymous - employees who fear their manager might see their responses rate engagement and satisfaction higher than their private views would suggest.

Reduction Strategies

Effective strategies for reducing social desirability bias include guaranteeing and visibly communicating anonymity; using self-administered rather than interviewer-administered modes; using indirect questioning formats that ask about others' behaviour rather than the respondent's own; placing sensitive questions later in the survey after rapport is established; using forced-choice formats that pair two equally desirable alternatives; and normalising non-idealistic responses through question framing ("Some people find it difficult to exercise regularly. How many times per week do you currently exercise?").

Frequently Asked Questions

Can social desirability bias be eliminated from surveys?+

Social desirability bias cannot be eliminated entirely, but it can be substantially reduced through survey mode selection, guaranteed anonymity, careful question wording, and indirect questioning techniques.

Does anonymity completely eliminate social desirability bias?+

Not completely. Research shows that even in fully anonymous online surveys, social desirability bias persists for topics connected to strong social norms or moral identity. However, genuine anonymity substantially reduces the bias.

How does social desirability affect employee engagement surveys?+

Employee engagement surveys that are not fully anonymous consistently show inflated engagement scores compared to anonymous versions. Employees who are concerned their responses might be attributed to them rate satisfaction and engagement more positively than their actual views reflect.

Is social desirability bias a form of lying?+

Not intentionally in most cases. Social desirability bias is an automatic psychological response to perceived social judgment, not a deliberate attempt to deceive. Respondents are often unaware their answers are being influenced by social norms rather than their genuine views.
