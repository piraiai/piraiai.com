What is a Semantic Differential Scale? Survey Design Guide

Arun Raj•July 18, 2026•3 min read•Glossary
Definition

A semantic differential scale is a rating scale that uses pairs of opposite adjectives (bipolar adjective pairs) at either end of a continuum, asking respondents to indicate where their perception falls between the two poles on a 5-point, 7-point, or numeric scale.

How Semantic Differential Scales Work

A semantic differential scale was developed by Charles Osgood in the 1950s to measure the connotative meaning of concepts. Each item presents a pair of opposite adjectives - such as "Innovative" to "Traditional" or "Warm" to "Cold" - at the ends of a scale with typically five or seven unlabelled positions between them. Respondents mark the position that best reflects their perception of the concept being rated.

Unlike Likert scales, which measure agreement or disagreement with a statement, semantic differential scales measure the position of an attitude or perception on a bipolar dimension. The respondent is not agreeing or disagreeing - they are locating their perception in a conceptual space defined by opposite poles.

Semantic Differential vs Likert Scale

  • Likert scales measure agreement with statements ("I find the product easy to use") on a Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree continuum.
  • Semantic differential scales locate a perception between opposite poles ("Easy to use - Difficult to use") without requiring engagement with a statement.
  • Semantic differential scales are particularly suited to measuring brand perceptions and product personality where the bipolar structure captures the dimensional quality of the perception better than agree-disagree statements.
  • Likert scales are better for measuring frequency, intensity, or agreement with specific propositions.

Applications in Survey Research

Semantic differential scales are most commonly used in brand perception research, product attribute assessment, advertising effectiveness research, and attitude measurement in social and political research.

In platform terms, semantic differential scales are implemented as rating scale questions with custom bipolar labels at each end. In Qualtrics, the Slider or Scale question types support custom end labels. Frequently Asked Questions

How many scale points should a semantic differential scale have?+

Seven-point scales are the most common, providing enough granularity to capture nuanced perceptions. Five-point scales are used when simplicity is prioritised. The scale points are typically left unlabelled with only the two poles labelled.

When should I use a semantic differential scale instead of a Likert scale?+

Use semantic differential scales when measuring brand or product perceptions that are inherently bipolar (modern vs traditional, warm vs cold), or when the agree-disagree format feels unnatural for the construct being measured.

Is a semantic differential scale the same as a bipolar scale?+

Yes. The terms are often used interchangeably. Bipolar scale refers to any scale with opposite poles at each end. 'Semantic differential' specifically refers to the Osgood methodology using adjective pairs to measure meaning.

Can semantic differential scales be used in mobile surveys?+

Semantic differential scales can be challenging on mobile because the bipolar label presentation requires horizontal space. A horizontal radio button or slider format with the two labels at each end works reasonably well on most modern mobile screens.
