How to Write 360 Feedback Survey Questions: Examples and Guide

Arun Raj•July 18, 2026•3 min read•Glossary
Definition

A 360 degree feedback survey collects structured feedback on an individual's behaviours, skills, and leadership effectiveness from multiple rater groups - typically the individual themselves (self-rating), direct manager, peers, and direct reports - enabling a multi-perspective view of development areas.

Essential Questions to Include

360 feedback surveys are organised around behavioural competencies relevant to the individual's role and level. A typical 360 for a mid-level manager covers 5-8 competency areas with 3-5 behaviourally anchored questions per competency, plus one or two open-text development questions. The total survey length should not exceed 30 minutes for any rater group.

Communication
"[Name] communicates ideas clearly and concisely to different audiences." (5-point frequency scale: Never to Always)
"[Name] listens actively and demonstrates understanding before responding."
"[Name] provides clear and timely updates to stakeholders."

Leadership and direction-setting
"[Name] articulates a clear direction and priorities for the team."
"[Name] makes decisions in a timely manner even when facing ambiguity."
"[Name] takes responsibility for outcomes, including when things go wrong."

Developing others (manager-rated questions, direct reports only)
"[Name] provides regular feedback that helps me improve my performance."
"[Name] actively supports my professional development."
"[Name] delegates effectively and trusts me to make decisions within my remit."

Collaboration
"[Name] works effectively across team boundaries to achieve shared goals."
"[Name] contributes constructively to group discussions and decisions."

Open-text development questions (2 maximum)
"What does [Name] do particularly well that they should continue doing?"
"What is the one thing [Name] could do differently to be more effective?"
These two questions (continue / change) produce the most actionable 360 feedback because they are specific and bounded.

Question Wording Best Practices

360 feedback questions must be behaviourally anchored - describing specific observable behaviours rather than traits or personality attributes. "Communicates clearly" is behavioural; "is a good communicator" is a trait judgment. Behaviourally anchored questions are more reliable because different raters can apply the same behavioural standard; trait judgments vary with the rater's personal definition of "good."

Write questions in the third person using the ratee's name placeholder: "[Name] demonstrates..." rather than "How well does [Name]..." or "Does [Name]..." Third-person declarative statements reduce acquiescence bias in 360 surveys more effectively than question formats.

Use a frequency scale (Never / Rarely / Sometimes / Usually / Always) rather than a quality scale (Poor / Below average / Average / Above average / Excellent) for behaviourally anchored items. Frequency scales ask raters to report what they observe, not to evaluate quality, which produces more consistent inter-rater scores and more specific feedback.

Platform Configuration

Qualtrics: Use Qualtrics 360 (part of the EmployeeXM suite) for full 360 administration including rater nomination, multi-rater survey distribution, and roll-up reporting by rater group. For organisations without the 360 module, a standard Qualtrics survey can be configured with embedded data for ratee name and rater relationship, enabling separate analysis by rater group in dashboards.

Microsoft Forms: Microsoft Forms can support 360 feedback for small teams, but lacks the rater nomination workflow and multi-rater roll-up reporting of dedicated 360 platforms. Use a separate form instance per ratee with the ratee name embedded in the form title. Collect rater relationship in the first question and segment responses manually in Excel.

Google Forms: Similar to Microsoft Forms, Google Forms can support basic 360 feedback but requires manual administration and analysis. Best suited for small organisations or team leaders running informal development feedback cycles rather than formal performance 360 programmes.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using trait rather than behavioural anchors. Questions like "Is [Name] a strong leader?" or "How would you rate [Name]'s emotional intelligence?" invite subjective trait judgments that vary widely between raters. Replace with specific behavioural statements: "[Name] remains calm and constructive when facing setbacks."

Not ensuring anonymity for direct reports. If direct reports believe their feedback can be attributed to them individually, they will inflate their ratings to protect the relationship. Minimum anonymity thresholds (typically 3 or more direct report responses before the group data is shared) must be enforced and communicated to all participants before the survey launches.

Including too many competencies. A 360 survey covering 10+ competency areas with 4 questions each exceeds 40 questions and takes 30+ minutes. Rater fatigue reduces data quality significantly after 20-25 questions. Focus on 5-7 competencies most critical for the ratee's current role and development goals.

Using 360 data for performance evaluation. 360 feedback data is most valid and useful as a development tool. Using multi-rater scores for compensation or promotion decisions creates incentives for political rating behaviour that undermine the accuracy of the data. Keep 360 data separate from formal performance appraisal systems.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many questions should a 360 feedback survey have?+

20-30 questions is the recommended range for a 360 feedback survey. Surveys with more than 35 questions show significant rater fatigue and reduced response quality, particularly for raters completing 360 surveys for multiple colleagues simultaneously. Focus on the highest-signal behaviours for the ratee's current role.

What scale should 360 feedback surveys use?+

A 5-point frequency scale (Never / Rarely / Sometimes / Usually / Always) is recommended for behaviourally anchored 360 items because it asks raters to report observable behaviour rather than make quality judgments. Include a 'Not observed / Don't know' option to allow raters to skip items for behaviours they have not had the opportunity to observe.

How many raters should be included in a 360?+

A typical 360 includes self, 1 manager, 3-5 peers, and 3-5 direct reports (for managers). Minimum rater counts for anonymous reporting are typically set at 3 for peer and direct report groups. Fewer than 3 raters in a group means the anonymity threshold cannot be met and that group's data should not be shown individually.
