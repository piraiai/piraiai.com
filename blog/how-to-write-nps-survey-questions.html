How to Write NPS Survey Questions: Examples and Platform Guide

Arun Raj•July 18, 2026•3 min read•Glossary
Definition

An NPS survey asks customers how likely they are to recommend a product, service, or organisation on a 0-10 scale. The survey typically contains one mandatory rating question and one open-text follow-up, making question wording precision especially critical.

Essential Questions to Include

An NPS survey is intentionally short. The standard structure is one rating question and one follow-up. Adding more questions is possible for transactional NPS but each additional question reduces response rates.

1. The NPS rating question (mandatory)
"On a scale of 0 to 10, how likely are you to recommend [Company / Product / Service] to a friend or colleague?"
Scale: 0 = Not at all likely, 10 = Extremely likely. Do not alter this wording or scale.

2. The follow-up open-text question (strongly recommended)
"What is the main reason for your score?"
This is the most important diagnostic question in any NPS programme. Without it, you know your score but not what is driving it.

3. Category driver question (optional, transactional NPS only)
"Which of the following best describes your main reason for the score you gave?" with a predefined list of drivers (e.g. Product quality, Customer service, Price, Delivery speed). This enables coded driver analysis without manual text categorisation.

4. Segment identifier (optional)
"How long have you been a customer of [Company]?" as a single-select question. This enables NPS analysis by customer tenure segment without requiring a CRM join.

Question Wording Best Practices

The NPS question wording is standardised for benchmarking purposes - do not change "friend or colleague" to "others" or "peers," as this alters the social reference point and produces scores that diverge from published benchmarks. The object of recommendation (company, product, or specific service) should be clearly specified rather than generic.

For the follow-up open-text question, "What is the main reason for your score?" outperforms "Why did you give us this score?" because it is neutral rather than assumptive. Avoid "What could we do better?" as a standalone follow-up - it only captures improvement areas from Detractors and Passives, missing the advocacy drivers from Promoters.

If you add a conditional follow-up - different questions for Promoters vs Detractors - use display logic rather than separate surveys. Ask Promoters "What do you value most about [Company]?" and Detractors "What would need to change to improve your score?"

Platform Configuration

Qualtrics: Use the native NPS question type (found under Scale questions). This automatically renders the 0-10 scale with correct labelling and enables NPS score calculation in dashboards. Add a Text Entry question with display logic set to "always display" for the follow-up. If adding Promoter/Detractor conditional follow-ups, use display logic conditions on the NPS question field value: 0-6 for Detractor questions, 9-10 for Promoter questions.

SurveyMonkey: Use the Net Promoter Score question type. The follow-up open text question is available as a standard text question. SurveyMonkey's NPS reports calculate and display the score automatically.

Google Forms: Google Forms does not have a native NPS question type. Use a Linear Scale question (0 to 10) with labels added at each end. The NPS score must be calculated manually from exported data. For programmes requiring automated NPS calculation, Google Forms is not the recommended platform.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Changing the scale to 1-10. This is the most common NPS configuration error. The 0-10 scale is not arbitrary - the scoring algorithm (0-6 as Detractors) is calibrated to the 11-point scale. A 1-10 scale changes how Detractors are calculated and makes benchmarking impossible.

Adding too many questions. An NPS survey longer than three questions shows measurably lower response rates. Every additional question beyond the core two should be justified by a specific analysis need.

Asking the follow-up before the rating. Always place the rating question first. Asking for reasons before the score primes respondents to think analytically about their rating rather than giving an instinctive loyalty signal.

Using NPS for internal attribution. NPS measures overall loyalty, not departmental performance. Attributing NPS scores to specific teams or agents without a clear causal link between the team's activity and the survey trigger produces misleading performance data.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many questions should an NPS survey have?+

The standard NPS survey has two questions: the 0-10 rating question and one open-text follow-up. Transactional NPS surveys can include up to three or four questions without significant response rate impact. Relationship NPS surveys should stay at two questions.

Can I add custom questions to an NPS survey?+

Yes, but each additional question reduces response rates. Add questions only where the data will directly inform decisions. Driver category questions (selecting from a predefined list) add less friction than additional open-text questions.

How do I write the NPS follow-up question?+

The most effective wording is neutral and open: 'What is the main reason for your score?' Avoid leading variations like 'What could we do better?' which only captures improvement areas, or 'Why did you give us that score?' which sounds defensive.
