How to Write Onboarding Survey Questions: Examples and Guide

Arun Raj•July 18, 2026•3 min read•Glossary
Definition

Employee onboarding surveys measure how well the onboarding experience prepares new hires for their role and integrates them into the organisation's culture. Well-designed onboarding surveys are deployed at multiple touchpoints (day 1, 30 days, 90 days) to capture the full arc of the new hire experience.

Essential Questions to Include

Onboarding surveys are most effective when deployed at three distinct touchpoints: at the end of week 1 (first impressions), at 30 days (initial role clarity and team integration), and at 90 days (job satisfaction and intent to stay). Each survey should be short - 8-12 questions - to maintain high response rates among busy new hires.

Week 1: First impressions
"I felt welcomed and supported on my first day." (5-point Likert)
"I have been given the equipment and access I need to start my role." (5-point Likert)
"My role has been clearly explained to me." (5-point Likert)
"Is there anything missing from your first week that would have helped you get started?" (open text)

30 days: Role clarity and integration
"I understand what is expected of me in my role." (5-point Likert)
"I feel like a valued member of my team." (5-point Likert)
"My manager has provided me with the guidance I need to succeed in my role." (5-point Likert)
"The onboarding training I have received has prepared me to do my job effectively." (5-point Likert)

90 days: Satisfaction and retention
"My role is what I expected it to be when I accepted the offer." (5-point Likert)
"I see a clear path for my growth and development at [Company]." (5-point Likert)
"I plan to still be working at [Company] in 12 months." (5-point Likert)
"What is the one thing [Company] could do to improve the onboarding experience?" (open text)

Question Wording Best Practices

Onboarding survey questions should be specific to the stage of the onboarding journey. "I feel settled into my role" is not meaningful at week 1 but is appropriate at 90 days. Align question content to what has actually happened at each survey touchpoint - asking about manager development conversations in week 1 before they have occurred produces invalid data.

New hires have high social desirability pressure in their first 90 days. They are building relationships with their manager and team and may be reluctant to report negative experiences honestly. Communicate anonymity clearly and explicitly in the survey invitation. If the survey is not anonymous, frame sensitive questions as forward-looking improvements rather than retrospective complaints: "What would most improve your experience in the next 30 days?" rather than "What has been difficult in your first 30 days?"

Platform Configuration

Qualtrics: Set up a Qualtrics Workflow triggered by the HRIS system at each onboarding milestone (day 7, day 30, day 90). Pass the employee ID, manager name, department, and start date as embedded data for segmented analysis. Use the same question wording across all three waves but adjust which questions appear using display logic based on an embedded data field for survey wave.

Microsoft Forms: Create three separate Microsoft Forms - one per onboarding wave. Connect Microsoft Forms to Power Automate to trigger survey invitations from the HRIS system at the correct milestone. For organisations using Microsoft 365 widely, Forms integrates cleanly with Teams and SharePoint for result sharing.

Google Forms: Create three separate Google Forms for each onboarding wave. Schedule send via Google Calendar or connect to an HRIS integration through Zapier. Google Forms works well for small organisations; larger organisations benefit from Qualtrics or Microsoft Forms for HRIS trigger integration and dashboard reporting.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Deploying a single survey at the wrong time. A single 90-day onboarding survey misses the week 1 equipment and access issues that could be fixed immediately. A single week 1 survey misses the 90-day retention signal that predicts first-year attrition. The three-wave approach captures the full onboarding journey and enables targeted interventions at each stage.

Surveying but not acting on results. If the 30-day survey shows that new hires consistently report unclear role expectations, and the next cohort experiences the same issue, new hires will notice. Onboarding survey results should feed directly into onboarding programme improvements with visible changes communicated to new hire cohorts.

Comparing 30-day scores to 90-day scores without context. Satisfaction typically dips at 90 days as the honeymoon period ends and job reality sets in. A decline in satisfaction scores from 30 to 90 days is not necessarily a programme failure - it is the baseline pattern. Track 90-day scores over time across cohorts rather than comparing 30-day to 90-day within a cohort.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many questions should an onboarding survey have?+

8-12 questions per onboarding wave (week 1, 30 days, 90 days). Shorter surveys for new hires reduce the burden during a period when they are already cognitively overloaded with new information. The three-wave approach gives you comprehensive data without requiring any single survey to be long.

Should onboarding surveys be anonymous?+

Yes where possible. New hires in their first 90 days have strong social desirability pressure and are unlikely to report problems honestly if their responses can be attributed to them. If full anonymity is not operationally possible, use aggregated reporting that does not surface individual responses to managers.

When should the 30-day onboarding survey be sent?+

Send the 30-day survey on working day 28-30, giving new hires 2-3 working days to complete it before the reminder. Sending too early (before day 25) means some new hires have not yet had the experiences the questions ask about. Sending after day 35 misses the window where problems can still be addressed early.
