How to Write Employee Engagement Survey Questions: Examples and Guide

Arun Raj•July 18, 2026•3 min read•Glossary
Definition

An employee engagement survey measures the emotional commitment, motivation, and connection that employees feel toward their organisation, role, and colleagues. Effective question design captures the key drivers of engagement rather than satisfaction or happiness alone.

Essential Questions to Include

A robust employee engagement survey covers the core engagement constructs identified in research: pride, advocacy, commitment, and discretionary effort. A typical annual engagement survey runs 30-50 questions; a pulse survey 8-15. The following are the highest-signal questions from each engagement dimension.

Advocacy and pride
"I would recommend [Company] as a great place to work." (5-point Likert: Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree)
"I am proud to work for [Company]."

Commitment and retention intent
"I plan to still be working at [Company] in two years' time."
"I rarely think about leaving [Company] to work somewhere else."

Discretionary effort
"I am willing to go beyond what is expected of me to help [Company] succeed."
"I feel motivated to do more than is required of me in my role."

Manager effectiveness
"My manager gives me the feedback I need to do my best work."
"My manager treats me with respect."

Role clarity and growth
"I know what is expected of me in my role."
"I have opportunities to develop my skills and grow at [Company]."

Psychological safety
"I feel comfortable raising concerns or questions with my team."
"My opinions are valued by my team."

Question Wording Best Practices

Employee engagement questions should be behavioural and specific rather than attitudinal and vague. "I understand how my work contributes to [Company]'s goals" produces more actionable data than "I feel my work is meaningful" because it points to a specific HR intervention (role clarity communication) rather than a diffuse feeling.

Use a consistent 5-point Likert scale throughout the survey. Mixing frequency scales ("Never" to "Always"), agreement scales ("Strongly Disagree" to "Strongly Agree"), and satisfaction scales within the same survey confuses respondents and complicates analysis. Choose one scale and apply it to all rated items.

Include at least two reverse-worded items to detect straight-lining. "I often feel disconnected from my team" alongside positively worded items flags respondents who are selecting the same option for every question. For surveys where anonymity is critical, communicate this explicitly in the survey introduction - it directly affects response honesty on sensitive items.

Platform Configuration

Qualtrics: Use the Matrix Table question type for Likert scale batteries, grouping related items by construct (manager, role clarity, wellbeing). Use blocks with randomised presentation to reduce order effects across respondents. Enable anonymity settings under Survey Options and display the anonymity guarantee in the survey header. For eNPS, add a separate NPS question type at the end.

Microsoft Forms: Use the Rating question type (1-5 or 1-10) for individual items and the Likert question type for matrix batteries. Microsoft Forms supports basic branching but lacks the advanced display logic needed for conditional follow-up questions. For large-scale annual engagement surveys, Qualtrics or Glint is typically more appropriate.

Google Forms: Use the Linear Scale or Multiple Choice Grid for Likert batteries. Google Forms does not support anonymity controls at the platform level - if anonymity is required, ensure the survey is not distributed via a personalised link or Google account login.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Measuring satisfaction instead of engagement. "How happy are you at work?" is a satisfaction question, not an engagement question. Engagement measures behavioural intent (would you recommend working here? do you plan to stay?) and effort (do you go beyond what is required?), not emotional state.

Using double-barreled questions. "My manager communicates clearly and treats me fairly" is two questions. Split every "and" construction into separate items.

Not acting on results. The biggest driver of low response rates in future engagement surveys is a perception that previous surveys produced no change. Include a commitment to share results and action plans in the survey communication, and follow through within 90 days.

Surveying too frequently without feedback loops. Monthly pulse surveys without visible action create cynicism faster than annual surveys with clear follow-through. Match survey frequency to the organisation's capacity to respond to findings.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many questions should an employee engagement survey have?+

Annual engagement surveys typically run 30-50 questions covering all major engagement dimensions. Pulse surveys should be 8-15 questions focused on a specific area or a rotating subset of core engagement items. Surveys longer than 50 questions show significant completion rate drops and straight-lining in later matrix batteries.

Should employee engagement surveys be anonymous?+

Yes, in almost all cases. Non-anonymous engagement surveys consistently show inflated scores because employees are concerned about manager attribution of responses. The anonymity guarantee should be explicit and credible - ideally enforced at the platform level, not just promised in the email invitation.

How often should employee engagement surveys be conducted?+

Annual surveys work well for baseline measurement and year-over-year tracking. Quarterly or monthly pulse surveys work well for monitoring progress on specific action areas between annual surveys. The critical factor is not frequency but whether results are acted on and communicated back to employees.
