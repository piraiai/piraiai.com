How to Write Academic Research Survey Questions: Design Guide

Arun Raj•July 18, 2026•3 min read•Glossary
Definition

Academic research surveys are designed to measure constructs with precision and rigour, meeting the validity, reliability, and ethical standards required by Institutional Review Boards. Question design in academic surveys prioritises measurement accuracy, informed consent, and the fidelity of validated instruments over brevity or respondent convenience.

Essential Questions to Include

Academic research surveys are typically built around validated instruments - scales with published reliability and validity evidence. The survey structure includes an informed consent section, screening questions, the validated instrument(s), demographic questions, and a debrief section for sensitive topics.

Informed consent (mandatory for human subjects research)
The consent section is not a survey question but a required acknowledgment. It describes the study purpose, participation time, risks and benefits, data storage, and the right to withdraw. Most IRB-approved online surveys display the consent as the first page with a mandatory acknowledgment before participants can proceed.

Screening questions (if applicable)
"Have you been diagnosed with [condition] by a licensed healthcare provider?" (Yes / No / Prefer not to say)
Screen for eligibility criteria before the main instrument. Use skip logic to terminate ineligible participants after the screening section.

Validated instrument items (core of the survey)
Use the exact wording and scale from the published validation study. Even minor rewording can affect validity. For example, the PHQ-9 depression screen uses specific time-frame wording ("Over the last 2 weeks, how often...") that must not be altered.

Demographics (standard set for academic research)
Age (exact year of birth or age band) / Gender identity (including non-binary options and prefer not to say) / Race and ethnicity (following NIH categories for US-funded research) / Highest education level / Employment status / Annual household income (banded)
Place demographics at the end of the survey to avoid priming identity-based response patterns in the main instrument.

Question Wording Best Practices

For validated instruments, the primary rule is exact replication. Do not simplify, shorten, or modernise the wording of validated scale items. The published reliability and validity evidence is specific to the exact item wording used in the validation study. Any modification requires a new validation study before the adapted instrument can be used in published research.

When writing custom items to supplement validated scales, follow psychometric scale development principles: items should be declarative statements (not questions) with a consistent response scale, written in the first person ("I feel..." rather than "Do you feel..."), and reviewed for face and content validity by subject matter experts before fielding.

Sensitive topics in academic research require particular care. Questions about trauma, mental health, substance use, sexuality, or criminal history must be preceded by a content warning, include a "prefer not to answer" option on all individual items (not just sections), and be followed by a debrief providing resources if the questions caused distress.

Platform Configuration

REDCap: REDCap is the standard platform for clinical and health sciences research surveys. Its data dictionary format defines every field name, type, label, choice options, and validation rule. Use calculated fields for composite score computation (PHQ-9 total score, GAD-7 total score). Enable survey queue for multi-instrument longitudinal studies. REDCap's audit trail and access controls support 21 CFR Part 11 compliance for clinical trials.

Qualtrics: Qualtrics is commonly used for social science and psychology research. Use the Scoring feature to automatically calculate composite scores for validated instruments. Set up anonymisation at the survey level for studies where no identifying data is required. For IRB submissions, use Qualtrics' HIPAA-compliant environment if the study involves health data.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Modifying validated instrument wording. Even small changes - replacing "often" with "frequently" or adding a qualifier - invalidate the published psychometric evidence. If modification is necessary for your population, report this as a limitation and conduct a pilot study to assess the impact on reliability.

Using the wrong reference period. Many validated instruments specify an exact time frame ("In the past two weeks...", "During the past month..."). Using the wrong time frame changes what the instrument measures and breaks the link to published norms.

Placing demographics before the main instrument. Placing age, gender, or race questions before the main instrument can prime identity-based responding (stereotype threat, social comparison). Standard practice is to collect demographics at the end of the survey.

Not piloting before data collection. A pilot study with 10-20 participants from the target population identifies comprehension issues, skipped items, and technical problems before they affect the main data collection. Piloting is especially important for populations with lower survey literacy or where cognitive load is a concern.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do academic survey questions need IRB approval?+

Yes, for research involving human participants at institutions receiving US federal funding, IRB (Institutional Review Board) or ethics board approval is required before data collection. The survey instrument, consent procedures, and data storage plan are all reviewed as part of the IRB submission.

Can I change the wording of a validated survey instrument?+

Minor changes require careful consideration and should be reported as a limitation. Significant changes invalidate the published psychometric evidence and require a new validation study. Always cite the original instrument source and note any modifications in your methods section.

What is the best platform for academic survey research?+

REDCap is the standard for clinical and health sciences research due to its data dictionary structure, audit trail, and compliance features. Qualtrics is commonly used for social science and psychology research due to its advanced logic, scoring, and distribution features. The choice depends on the research context, institutional access, and compliance requirements.
